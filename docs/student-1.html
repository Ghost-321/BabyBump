What to expect

What will happen to your body

Although a lot of people think not many changes happen in the first trimester, there are many changes that do occur. You will likely experience rapid changes:

  • Hormone Surge:
    • The hormone progesterone rises quickly, causing extreme tiredness.
  • Breast changes: Breasts may become tender, swollen, and heavier. Your areolas (the skin around your nipples) may darken.
  • Frequent Urination: Increased blood volume means your kidneys process extra fluid, putting pressure on your bladder.
  • Food Changes: You may develop strong cravings or sudden aversions to certain smells and tastes.
  • Morning Sickness:s Nausea and vomiting can strike at any time of day, triggered by changing hormone levels.

Baby's development

During the 12 weeks your baby will expertience vital chnages in their growth, growing from just a poppy seed to about 7.62cm

  • Weeks 1-4: Implantation occurs, and the neural tube (which becomes the brain and spinal cord) begins to form.
  • Weeks 5-8: The heart begins to beat. Basic facial features start to appear, and tiny limb buds grow into arms and legs.
  • Weeks 9-12: The embryo is now officially called a fetus. Fingers, toes, eyelids, and genital organs develop. Your baby can even start to make small movements.

Key take away

Physical changes

During the first trimester, the body undergoes significant hormonal and physical changes to support the growing pregnancy. Rising levels of hormones such as progesterone and human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) can cause tiredness, breast tenderness, frequent urination, bloating, constipation, and mood changes. Some women may notice mild weight gain or even weight loss if morning sickness is severe. The uterus begins to expand, although the pregnancy may not yet be visible. Every pregnancy is different, so the type and intensity of symptoms can vary from person to person.

Healthcare

Regular prenatal healthcare is essential during the first trimester to support the health of both the mother and the developing baby. The first prenatal appointment usually includes confirmation of the pregnancy, a review of the mother's medical history, a physical examination, blood and urine tests, and an estimated due date. Healthcare providers may recommend prenatal vitamins containing folic acid to help prevent neural tube defects, along with advice on healthy eating, exercise, and avoiding alcohol, smoking, and harmful substances. Early prenatal care also allows healthcare professionals to identify and manage any potential health concerns, helping to promote a healthy pregnancy.

When to get help

You should seek immediate medical attention if you experience severe abdominal pain, heavy vaginal bleeding, or a fever over 38°C, as these can indicate ectopic pregnancy, miscarriage, or infection. Contact your healthcare provider promptly if you have severe, persistent vomiting that prevents you from keeping liquids down for 24 hours, which can cause dangerous dehydration. Additionally, reach out to your doctor or midwife right away if you notice sudden, severe swelling in your hands or face, extreme headaches, vision changes, or if you experience severe feelings of depression, anxiety, or panic.
