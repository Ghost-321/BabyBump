What to expect

What happens to your body?

  • The third trimester is a combination of excitement, discomfort and full of rapid changes. This trimester brings major changes, stronger symptoms and final preparation for labour. As the baby grows rapidly your body shifts and changes in many ways. Such as your belly size growing, shortness of breath, back and pelvic pain, swelling, heartburn and trouble sleeping.

    • Baby's development

  • At this stage the baby rapidly gains weight doubling or sometimes even triplets in size. The baby starts moving more frequently kicking and stretching, The baby's lungs mature preparing for it to breathe air. The baby's brain develops, making major growth in neural connections.

    • Medical appointments & tests

  • Make sure to have checkups every 7-14 days Make sure to frequently do growth scars to make sure the baby's weight and size is normal. Make sure to blood pressure tests regularly as high blood pressure can affect the baby’s oxygen and nutrients intake.

Key take away

physical changes

In the third trimester, your body goes through its biggest changes as your baby grows rapidly and your organs shift to make space. You may feel more breathless, heavier, and tired because the uterus pushes upward against your lungs and stomach, while hormones loosen your joints and ligaments, causing back pain, hip discomfort, and pelvic pressure. Swelling in your feet and hands becomes more common due to increased blood volume, and you might experience heartburn, constipation, frequent urination, and trouble sleeping as your digestive system and bladder are compressed. These changes are normal, but sudden swelling, severe headaches, or reduced baby movements should be checked by a healthcare professional.

Healthcare

Your healthcare in the third trimester becomes more frequent and more detailed because this is when complications can appear suddenly. You’ll usually have check‑ups every 1–2 weeks, where your midwife or doctor monitors blood pressure, baby’s growth, baby’s movements, and your symptoms. They may do growth scans, urine tests, and a Group B Strep test around 36–37 weeks. These visits help detect issues like pre‑eclampsia, gestational diabetes complications, low amniotic fluid, or slowed baby growth early, so they can act quickly if needed. Healthcare providers also help you prepare for labour by discussing birth plans, pain relief options, and signs of labour, making sure you and your baby stay safe as delivery approaches.

When to get help

You need help any time something feels sudden, severe, or unusual. This includes strong headaches, blurred vision, sudden swelling in your hands, face, or feet, reduced baby movements, bleeding, severe abdominal pain, or regular painful contractions before 37 weeks. You should also get help if you notice fluid leaking (possible waters breaking), chest pain, shortness of breath that feels worse than normal, or if you simply feel something isn’t right. These signs can point to conditions like pre‑eclampsia, preterm labour, or problems with the placenta, and getting checked quickly keeps both you and your baby safe.
